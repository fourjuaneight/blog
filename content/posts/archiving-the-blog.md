I stared at my code editor for twenty minutes last Tuesday. Not because I was debugging something or working through some problem; I was trying to write a blog post about a shell script I'd been using to batch-rename files. Twenty minutes of a blinking cursor, and all I could think was: *I do not care about this*. Not the script itself; the script is fine, it works, it saved me an afternoon. What I didn't care about was performing enthusiasm for it. Packaging it up in a tidy little post with a catchy title and a closing line about how automation is the future or whatever. I closed the tab, made a coffee, and sat with the uncomfortable realization that I've been feeling this way for a while now.

So here it is. I'm archiving this blog.

---

This site started the way a lot of developer blogs start, as a playground. I wanted to learn how static site generators worked, so I built one. Then I wanted to understand Hugo better, so I rebuilt it. Then I got into Tailwind, so I rebuilt it again. The writing was almost secondary; the blog was a sandbox where I could break things and push code and try weird ideas without a PM asking me to justify the sprint points. It was *fun*. The kind of fun where you lose track of time at 1 a.m. because you're trying to get a dark mode toggle to animate just right, and nobody's paying you for it, and that's exactly why it feels good.

I wrote about the tools I was using, the workflows I was building, the little automations that made my day smoother. And I meant every word of it. There was a period, maybe 2022 into early 2023, where I genuinely believed I'd be one of those people who blogs consistently. I wrote about [owning your data](/posts/on-owning-your-data/), about [coming back to blogging](/posts/betting-on-bloggin/), about [authenticity](/posts/authenticity-and-the-personal-blog/). I kept saying the quiet part out loud: *this matters, writing matters, having your own corner of the internet matters*.

I still believe all of that.

But belief and energy are two different currencies, and I've been running a deficit on the second one for longer than I'd like to admit.

---

Here's the thing about working in tech full-time, and I mean the corporate, enterprise, stand-up-at-9-a.m., Jira-ticket-grinding kind of tech; it colonizes your brain. Not in a dramatic, dystopian way. More like a slow leak. You spend eight, nine, ten hours a day thinking about code, about architecture, about deployment pipelines and incident reports and why that one microservice keeps timing out on Thursdays. And then someone, maybe yourself, maybe the little voice that remembers when this was fun, says, "Hey, you should write a blog post about that new CSS feature." And your entire nervous system recoils.

It's not burnout, exactly. Or maybe it is, the quiet kind that doesn't look like anything from the outside. You still ship code. You still show up. But the idea of coming home and *voluntarily* opening VS Code to write about development? It starts to feel like asking a line cook to go home and prepare a five-course meal for fun. You love cooking. You became a cook because you love cooking. But after ten hours on the line, you're eating cereal over the sink, and that's fine, actually.

The joy I used to get from writing about productivity hacks and tooling and "here's how I set up my terminal"; that joy didn't vanish overnight. It eroded. Slowly. Post by post, draft abandoned after draft abandoned. Each one a little harder to start, a little harder to finish, the gap between published pieces stretching wider until the gaps became the norm and the writing became the exception.

---

My relationship with technology has shifted, too, and I think that matters more than I initially wanted to admit. When I started this blog, I was wide-eyed about the web. Every new framework felt like a door opening. Every tool was a possibility. I wasn't cynical; I was *curious*, and curiosity is rocket fuel for a blog like this.

But these days? I look at the tech landscape and I mostly feel tired. Not of technology itself; I still love building things, still get a kick out of a well-structured function, still find genuine satisfaction in solving a hard problem. What exhausts me is the *culture* around it. The relentless hype cycles. The way every conversation eventually bends toward AI and whether it's going to [ruin everything](/posts/ai-is-ruining-the-internet/) or save everything, with no room for the more honest answer, which is: it's complicated, and I don't know, and I'm not sure anyone does. The LinkedIn-ification of developer discourse, where every blog post is secretly a personal brand play and every opinion is optimized for engagement.

I got into this because I liked making things on the internet. Somewhere along the way, "making things on the internet" became an industry with thought leaders and influencer-engineers and a content treadmill that never stops. And I just don't want to be on that treadmill. I never did. This blog was supposed to be the opposite of that. A place where I could be honest, be weird, write about [coffee](/posts/on-coffee/) and [video games](/posts/on-gaming/) and data hoarding in the same space where I wrote about pull requests and code abstraction. And for a while, it was.

But the version of me that wants to write isn't the version of me that wants to write about *this* anymore.

---

I want to be clear: this isn't a dramatic farewell. I'm not deleting anything. The posts stay up, every rambling essay, every hot take, and every piece that makes me wince a little when I reread it. That's the whole point of [archiving](/posts/is-stuff-online-worth-saving/), right? You preserve the thing as it was, not as you wish it had been.

And I'm not done writing. I'm really not. If anything, the urge to write has been *growing*; it's just pulling me in different directions. I want to write about the books I'm reading, about cycling, about the strange experience of getting older and realizing your hobbies are changing and that's not a failure, it's just life doing its thing. I want to write without the implicit expectation, self-imposed mostly, that every piece needs to tie back to web development or productivity or some lesson about the tech industry. I want to write about what I actually think about when I'm not working.

So there will probably be another blog. A different one, with a different shape, unburdened by the gravitational pull of my day job. I don't know what it'll look like yet. I don't know what I'll call it. I don't even know what stack I'll build it on, and for the first time in a long time, the not-knowing feels exciting rather than paralyzing.

---

If you've read any of this blog, any of these ramblings over the years, thank you. I mean that without a shred of performative gratitude. You didn't have to be here. The internet is vast and loud and full of things competing for your attention, and yet you spent some of it reading my thoughts about shell scripts and data ownership and whether stuff online is worth saving. That's a kind of generosity I never took for granted, even when I was bad at showing it by disappearing for months at a time.

This site was never big. It was never important in the way the internet measures importance, no viral posts, no massive subscriber count, no sponsorship deals. It was just mine. A weird little corner where I figured things out in public, changed my mind, and occasionally said something that resonated with a stranger. That's more than enough.

The blog is archived. The writer isn't.
